## How wealth clients actually use an advisor's website

The decision to hire a wealth advisor is rarely fast. From "I think we need to look at this" to a signed engagement letter is usually six to eighteen months. During that window, the prospect is doing two things on your website. They are trying to decide whether they trust the way you think, and they are quietly checking whether the structural details add up.

What they look for is specific. Are you a fiduciary, or do you only owe a suitability standard. Are you fee-only, or do you take commissions. Where are client assets actually held, because if the answer is "with us" they will close the tab. What is the minimum, because nobody at $4M wants a forty-minute discovery call with a firm that wants $250K relationships. What does the principal actually think about markets, taxes, and planning, because at this size every prospect is buying a brain, not a brand.

What loses the relationship is generic. Stock photography of a couple smiling at a sunset, vague language about "comprehensive wealth management," a calculator widget asking how much they have invested before the firm has said anything about itself. Anything that pattern-matches to a broker-dealer pretending to be a fiduciary, and the prospect is back on Google.

## What most advisor sites get wrong

I audit DFW advisor sites every month. The patterns are stubborn.

The fee schedule is hidden behind "contact us for a personalized fee proposal," which prospects read as "we want to figure out what we can charge you." The custodian relationship is invisible, even though Schwab or Fidelity custody is the single most important fraud-resistance signal a registered investment advisor can offer. The minimum is missing, so unqualified prospects waste time and qualified ones cannot self-select. The site uses "we" voice but never names a principal, so the trust signal that should anchor the page never lands.

Then there are the missing surfaces. No published quarterly commentary, even though every prospect is reading the firm's own writing as a proxy for how they think. No specialized practice areas, even though that is exactly how an executive with RSUs decides whether you understand their situation. No client portal in the header, which most clients use weekly. No Form CRS link, no Form ADV link, no fiduciary statement, no privacy notice, no plain-language compliance band. The footer says "registered investment advisor" and stops there.

Category truth: the DFW advisors who close $5M relationships from a website treat the page like a structural disclosure document with editorial typography. The rest treat it like a brochure that mentions "fiduciary" once.

## What your advisor site actually needs

Start with the navigation. Services, Fees, Philosophy, Team, Insights, Contact, Client Login. Not "Resources." Not "Wealth Insights powered by [parent firm]." A sticky discovery-call CTA, not a generic Contact button.

Below the hero, a fiduciary pledge written in first person and signed by the principal. "I am a fiduciary. I do not take commissions. My only revenue is from clients." That is the differentiator, and it should be the second thing the prospect reads. Below that, a Who I Serve section with a real client minimum and the two or three sub-segments the firm actually wants, so qualified prospects self-select and unqualified ones leave with their dignity. Below that, a specialized practice grid that proves technical depth: equity compensation, business sale planning, multi-generational transfer, tax coordination, estate coordination, charitable giving.

The fee schedule lives on the page, in a real table, with real numbers. Tiered AUM rates, planning-only retainer, what is included. Below it, a custodian band naming Schwab, Fidelity, and Pershing, with a one-sentence explanation that the firm does not take physical custody. A quarterly insights section publishes four substantive letters a year under the principal's byline, dated. Recognition is dated and specific, with a methodology disclaimer that signals the firm understands ranking compliance. A privacy and cybersecurity strip names the actual practices: encrypted document vault, annual penetration testing, multi-factor authentication. The contact path is a structured discovery call, not a generic form. Thirty minutes, no fee, no follow-up if it is not a fit. The footer carries Form ADV Part 2A, Form ADV Part 2B, Form CRS, privacy policy, fiduciary statement, SEC file number, CRD number, state notice filings, and the registration disclaimer.
