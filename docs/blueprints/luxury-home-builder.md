## How custom home buyers actually use your website

Custom home buyers are not in a hurry. The average commission cycle from "I think it's time to build" to "the contract is signed" runs 6 to 18 months. During that window the buyer is doing one thing on your website: deciding whether you build the way they want to live.

What they look for is specific. They want completed homes, not renderings. They want homes in their neighborhood, because Highland Park feels nothing like Plano feels nothing like Volk Estates. They want to know which architects you have worked with, because a builder is judged by the company they keep. They want the principal's name and face, because at this price point they are buying a relationship, not a service.

What makes them leave is generic. Stock photography, headlines about "dream homes," a "Get a Quote" button in the navigation. Anything that signals production builder dressed up as custom sends a $4M client back to Google in under thirty seconds.

## What most builder sites get wrong

The biggest miss in the DFW custom builder market is treating the website like a brochure when buyers treat it like a portfolio review. I audit these sites constantly. The patterns repeat.

Hero photos are renderings, not built work. Galleries are unlabeled, undated, and unsorted. Neighborhoods are nowhere on the site, even though buyers care about which builder works in which community more than almost anything else. Available inventory, including spec homes mid-construction, is missing entirely. Recognition (AIA awards, D Home features, NARI honors) is buried in a footer or absent. The principals are anonymous behind a faceless About Us.

Then there is the most expensive missing surface: a journal. Custom builder buyers spend months researching. They want to read what you think about hardware, about renovation versus new construction, about working with their architect. Sites that publish nothing leave that authority on the table for the builder down the street who does.

## What your site actually needs

Start with the navigation. Galleries, Communities, Available Homes, Studio (the team), Journal, Contact. No "Get a Quote" button. The signal at this tier is selectivity, not solicitation.

Galleries should be organized by residence and by neighborhood, with each project titled, dated, and credited to its architect. Communities is its own surface: the named neighborhoods you build in (Highland Park, Preston Hollow, Volk Estates, Bluffview), each with photo, project history, and count. Available Homes is real inventory: spec homes in build with status indicators, plus any lots held for the right architect-and-owner pairing. Recognition is a dedicated section listing the publications you have appeared in and the recent honors you have received, dated and specific.

The principal section names the founding builder, shows their face, and includes a personal letter signed at the end. Trust at this price point is bought with first-person specificity, not third-person credentials. A journal section publishes four to eight essays a year on construction practice, written in plain language, with opinions.

Compliance matters more than most builders think. Texas Residential Construction License number visible in the footer. AIA Dallas and NARI affiliations stated. Studio hours and address explicit. The contact path is direct: phone, email, and a contact section that promises a personal response from the principal within two business days, not a generic form that lands in a project manager's inbox.
