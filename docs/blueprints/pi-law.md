## How injury victims actually find a lawyer

The personal injury client is in the worst hour of their life. They were just rear-ended on Central Expressway. They are in the ER, or the parking lot of the ER, or the back seat of an Uber going home with a brace on. They are bleeding or scared, often both. They are not researching firms. They are searching from a phone with one hand, and they want to know four things in this exact order: do you take my case, what does it cost, can I talk to a real lawyer right now, and have you won cases like mine.

What converts is specific. A "no fee unless we win" badge above the fold, in plain language. A phone number that is the loudest element on the page. A 24/7 promise that is real, not aspirational, with a human answering at midnight. A form that takes ninety seconds and promises an attorney callback the same day, not a paralegal. A bilingual call-out, because in DFW a meaningful share of injury clients are Spanish-first and they are calling from the ER at 11pm. Real settlement numbers with the case type and the year attached.

What loses the case is generic. A contact form that promises a 24-hour response. Stock photography of a gavel. A "we fight for you" headline with no proof. Recognition badges with no years. No price language at all, so the client assumes it costs money to call and dials the next firm on the search results.

## What most PI law sites get wrong

I audit DFW personal injury sites every month. The patterns are stubborn.

The "no fee unless we win" promise, which is the entire pricing model of contingency-fee work, is buried in a footer or stated nowhere. The phone number is small in the header. The intake form lives on a separate Contact page reachable in two clicks instead of being on the home page. There is no Spanish call-out, even on firms whose actual client base is forty percent Spanish-speaking. Recognition is a wall of unread badges with no years and no peer-review explanation.

Then there are the missing surfaces. No multi-modal contact, even though a client with one good arm would rather text a photo of the wrecked car than dictate a phone call. No "what to do after an accident" content, even though that is the literal Google query that brings the highest-intent traffic in the category. No FAQ answering the questions every PI client Googles before they call. No office locations stated when the firm has three. No statute-of-limitations urgency stated anywhere. No State Bar advertising disclaimer or "prior results do not guarantee similar outcome" near the case results, which is both required and credibility-building.

Category truth: the PI firms that sign cases at midnight treat their website like an intake desk. The rest treat it like a TV-ad placeholder.

## What your PI firm site actually needs

Start at the very top. A red 24/7 strip above the nav with the phone as a tap-to-call link and a Spanish call-out next to it. The nav: Practice Areas, Results, After an Accident, Attorneys, FAQ, Free Consultation. A "Free Case Review" CTA in the upper right that stays visible.

The hero leads with a "no fee unless we win" badge above the headline. Below the headline, two CTAs side by side: Free Case Review and tap-to-call. Then a slim trust line with board certification and years in practice. Right after the credentials bar, an intake section with a real form (name, phone, case type, brief description) and a written promise that a licensed attorney calls back the same day, not a paralegal.

Results are not a vague "millions recovered" claim. They are a ledger of recent verdicts with year, case type, county, and dollar amount, then a grid of card-format result summaries with a sentence of context on each. Practice areas are detailed: auto, truck and 18-wheeler, motorcycle, pedestrian and bicycle, premises liability, work injury, wrongful death, dog bites, plus catastrophic injury and medical malpractice. Each as its own card.

Below that, a "what to do after an accident" section with seven numbered steps that doubles as the lead magnet for the email list. Then a recognition wall with eight peer-reviewed honors, year-stamped. Then a multi-modal contact strip (Call, Text, Online Form, Live Chat) where every channel reaches the same intake desk. Then office locations with addresses and direct numbers for each city served. Then an FAQ that answers the literal questions clients Google ("Do I have a case?", "What does it cost?", "What if I was partially at fault?", "What if the other driver had no insurance?", "Hablan Espanol?").

The footer carries the State Bar of Texas attorney advertising notice, the responsible-attorney name, the "past results do not guarantee a similar outcome" disclaimer, the principal office address, and a sticky mobile CTA bar that pins Call and Free Case Review to the bottom of the screen on every phone.
