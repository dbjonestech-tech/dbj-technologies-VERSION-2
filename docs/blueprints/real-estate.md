## How luxury buyers and sellers actually use an agent's website

A buyer at the four-million-dollar mark is not searching MLS. They are vetting an agent. They have already seen the public listings on Zillow, Redfin, and the brokerage syndication. By the time they land on your site, the question they are asking is not "what is for sale," it is "is this person someone I trust to walk me through the most personal financial transaction of my decade."

What converts is specific. Closed transactions with addresses, neighborhoods, and the year, not vague "career volume" totals. Active and recently contracted listings with clear status (Active, Under Contract, Just Sold), so the buyer can read the pace of your business in twenty seconds. Press placements with the publication and the year visible, not a dusty awards badge from 2018. A direct phone line and a personal email, not a contact form. The agent's name and face above any brokerage logo.

What loses the call is generic. A "Search MLS" button as the hero CTA. Stock photos of glassy high-rises that have nothing to do with the agent's actual market. A Compass or Sotheby's logo larger than the agent's name. A team page listing twelve people when the buyer is hiring one person. The buyer closes the tab and calls the agent their friend used last year.

## What most agent sites get wrong

I audit DFW agent sites every month. The patterns are stubborn.

The site reads as a brokerage marketing template, not a personal practice. The agent's recent closings are missing entirely, or hidden behind a "Sold" toggle that loads slowly and shows pixelated thumbnails. There is no neighborhood expertise on the page beyond a list of city pills, even though luxury buyers care about Highland Park versus Volk Estates more than almost anything else. The buyer's process and the seller's process are either absent or collapsed into one generic timeline that serves neither side.

Then there are the missing surfaces. No off-market or private list email capture, even though luxury inventory turns mostly off-market and capturing that email is the single highest-value form on the site. No concierge offering described in detail, even though staging, renovation coordination, and relocation network access are exactly what differentiates a $4M agent from a $400K agent. Press is reduced to logos with no year stamp, so a 2017 placement reads the same as a 2025 one. Compliance is buried: TREC license, equal housing, brokerage license, and MLS affiliation either missing or set in 8-point gray.

The category truth I see at every audit: the Dallas agents who hold seven-figure listings consistently treat their website like an introduction. The rest treat it like a brochure.

## What your agent site actually needs

Start with the navigation. Listings, Sold, Neighborhoods, About, Process, Concierge, Press, Contact. The brokerage affiliation sits to the right, quiet but present. The hero is a single considered photograph of the kind of home you actually represent, with a personal headline and a short stat line (career volume, transaction count, years in market) and one CTA: Begin a Conversation. No search bar.

About is a genuine first-person letter, signed by you, with credentials and license number visible at the foot. Listings shows three to six current homes with status glyphs (Active, Under Contract, Just Sold) so the page reads as a working business. Sold is a real list of recent closings with addresses, neighborhoods, year, and the side you represented, twenty-four months deep. Neighborhoods is its own surface: six entries minimum, each with a short editorial note and a current median price. The buyer's process and the seller's process live side by side as named, premium-feeling timelines, four steps each. Concierge is a real list of what you do beyond the transaction: off-market access, staging, renovation strategy, relocation, photography, trusted advisors.

Press is dated and recent: D Magazine, Wall Street Journal, Mansion Global, with the year visible. A private list email capture invites buyers and sellers into the off-market loop. The contact path is direct: a personal phone, a personal email, and a written response window from you, not your team. The footer carries TREC license number, brokerage name and license, MLS affiliation, equal housing opportunity, and the TREC Consumer Protection Notice, set quietly but clearly. Mobile gets every section to behave, because much of this is read from the back of a car between showings.
