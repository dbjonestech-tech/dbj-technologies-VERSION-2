## How diners actually use a restaurant website

The decision a diner makes on your website is small in scope and large in stakes. They have a Tuesday-night impulse, a Friday anniversary, a corporate dinner for nine, a Mother's Day brunch. They are not browsing. They want to know three things: what is the food, can they get a table, and is this the right room for the night ahead.

What converts is specific. A current menu with prices, dated this week, not a PDF from 2019. Photographs of the actual dining room and the actual food, not stock plates over linen. A reservation button that works in three taps from any page, including the bar program page and the private events page. Awards from the last twenty-four months, with the publication and the year, not a pile of unread badges.

What loses the table is generic. A "Coming Soon" menu page. A reservation flow that demands a phone call on a Saturday morning. A photo gallery of food the kitchen no longer serves. Hours that say "Open Daily" with no times. The diner closes the tab and books the place down the block whose website actually answered the question.

## What most restaurant sites get wrong

I audit Dallas restaurant sites every month. The patterns are stubborn.

Menus are PDFs that have not been updated since the chef changed. Reservations live behind one OpenTable link and nowhere else. The bar program, which often does forty percent of revenue at an upscale restaurant, gets a single sentence and no menu. Private dining, the highest-margin business in the building, hides behind a contact form with no room dimensions, no per-guest pricing, no named host, and no sample menu to forward to the boss.

Then there are the missing surfaces. No gift cards page, even though gift cards are near pure-margin revenue. No pantry or take-home product page, even when the kitchen makes a granola or a hot sauce that would sell. No preferred guest list to capture diners who already love the place but cannot get a Saturday-night table. No General Manager named, no Sommelier named, no Bar Director named, even though regulars come back specifically for those people.

The category truth I see at every audit: the Dallas restaurants that book solid every weekend treat their website like a host. The ones that do not treat it like a brochure.

## What a real restaurant site needs

Start at the top. A slim promo bar above the nav holds the time-sensitive moment: Mother's Day brunch, the truffle dinner, the New Year's seating. The nav is split: Menu, Wine, Bar, Pantry on the left, then the wordmark, then Private Events, Gift Cards, and a single high-contrast Reserve button that stays visible on every scroll of every page.

The menu lives on the page, not a PDF. It is dated. It updates when the chef changes a dish. The bar gets its own anchor with a real brand name, three signature cocktails with prices, and the bartender named. The pantry is a real shelf with real prices: a cocktail kit, granola, hot sauce, a holiday box. Gift cards have their own card with denominations and a same-day-email purchase path.

Private dining is two rooms minimum: a main private space with capacity and per-guest pricing, and a smaller chef's-counter or wine-room option at a different tier. The General Manager is named with a personal commitment ("I answer every inquiry within four business hours"). Dress code policy is linked. Sample menus are downloadable. The events email is dedicated.

Recognition is dated and recent: D Magazine, Eater, Texas Monthly, OpenTable, Wine Spectator, with the year visible. Hours break down by day with format annotations. A preferred guest list captures email for early-access seasonal changes and the occasional cancellation worth knowing about. The footer names the GM, trade association, accessibility, privacy, and the Instagram handle regulars actually follow. Mobile gets tap-to-call, a sticky Reserve, and a menu legible without zoom.
