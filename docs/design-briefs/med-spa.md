## How aesthetics patients actually use a med spa website

The aesthetics patient is researching, not panicking. The decision cycle runs four to twelve weeks from "I have been thinking about my forehead" to a first injectable visit. During that window the patient is doing one thing on your website: deciding whether you are the practice they trust to put a needle in their face.

What converts is specific. A named, board-certified physician with a face and a license number. A real treatment menu with real per-unit and per-syringe pricing, not a "schedule a consult to learn more" curtain. Before-and-after photographs taken in the same room with the same lighting. Reviews counted across the platforms patients actually check, Google and RealSelf in particular. A consultation that is described in concrete terms, free, thirty minutes, no obligation, with a sentence that says you will leave with a written plan and a price.

What loses the patient is generic. A "luxury wellness destination" headline with no physician named. A treatment list with descriptions and zero pricing. A gallery of stock images. A reviews badge that says "5 stars" with no platform and no count. A consult flow that demands a call during business hours when the patient is researching at 10pm on a Tuesday.

## What most med spa sites get wrong

I audit DFW med spa sites every month. The patterns are stubborn.

The Medical Director is invisible. The site lists "our team of expert injectors" without a single license number, NPI, or named physician. Pricing is hidden everywhere, even though the practices that book heavily publish it. Membership is missing entirely, even though monthly skincare clubs are the highest-margin recurring revenue line in the category. Specials are a permanent banner that says "Save on Botox" with no date, which patients have learned to ignore.

Then there are the missing surfaces. No financing call-out, even on $2,400 laser packages. No retail shelf for the SkinMedica or ZO regimens the patient already uses, leaving that revenue at Amazon. No safety language, no hyaluronidase mention, no aftercare line, no acknowledgment that medical aesthetics is medicine. No HIPAA notice in the footer. No state board, no license number, no NPI, despite all three being mandatory disclosures in most jurisdictions.

The category truth: the DFW med spas that book out their consult calendar treat the website like a transparent practice. The rest treat it like a salon brochure with serious treatments hidden inside.

## What your med spa site actually needs

Start at the top. A slim plum promo strip carries the dated event, Spring Botox Days with the per-unit price and the four-day window. The nav splits across the wordmark with Treatments, Pricing, Membership, Specials on one side and Results, Shop, Team, Contact on the other, with a high-contrast Book Consultation button that stays visible.

The hero is editorial and quiet, the physician named in the first paragraph with credentials. Below it, the per-area pricing menu publishes Tox per unit, filler per syringe, energy per session, IV per drip. A Society membership section runs a real monthly price with a transferable benefits card and a fineprint that names the cancellation terms. A Specials surface holds three dated, capped offers with redemption windows. A Before-and-After gallery shows three real cases with treatment notes and provider attribution.

The team section names a physician Medical Director with license number and NPI, a lead RN injector with state license number, and a licensed master esthetician with state credential. A Shop surface carries the brand shelf, ZO, SkinMedica, Obagi, Alastin, ISDIN, EltaMD, with online ordering and in-clinic pickup. A reviews strip names the platforms with the count and rating attached. A Safety section makes the medical part visible, physician oversight, sterile technique, hyaluronidase on site, aftercare direct line. Financing names CareCredit, Cherry, and Affirm with specific terms. The footer carries the Texas Medical Board oversight, the medical license number, the NPI, the HIPAA notice, and the Notice of Privacy Practices link.
