## How dental patients actually choose a practice

Most dental visits are not chosen, they are tolerated. The patient is in pain, has lost a filling, has a child screaming about a wiggly tooth, or has avoided the chair for years and finally promised a spouse they would call. They are searching from a phone with three things on their mind: can I be seen this week, will my insurance work here, and is this office going to talk down to me.

What converts is specific. A new patient price visible above the fold. A real list of carriers in network. A same-day emergency promise with a phone number that actually answers. Photographs of the actual office and the actual doctor, not stock teeth on a stock face. A monthly membership for the patient who does not have insurance and is tired of being penalized for it.

What loses the booking is generic. "We accept most insurance" without a list. A contact form in place of a phone number. A doctor bio with no name above the fold and no photo within reach. No pricing on cleanings. No mention of sedation when the search query was "dentist for anxiety."

## What most dental sites get wrong

I audit DFW dental sites every month. The patterns are stubborn.

The new patient special is missing or buried. The membership plan, which is the highest-margin recurring revenue line for any cash-pay patient, is absent or hidden behind "ask us about it." The insurance section says "we work with most plans" instead of naming Delta, BCBS, Cigna, Aetna, MetLife, Guardian, Humana, United Concordia. Financing is missing entirely on a $4,000 implant page. Sedation is mentioned in passing on a site that should be selling it as the answer to dental anxiety in the hero.

Then there are the missing surfaces. No smile gallery, even though the cosmetic dollar is the easiest to win when the work is shown. No office tour, even though the practice spent six figures on CEREC and 3D scanning that no patient knows about. No hygiene team named, even though most dental relationships are with the hygienist by year two. No multi-modal contact, even though the patient with a chipped front tooth would rather text a photo than call. No emergency promise, even though the highest-intent search of the day is "emergency dentist near me."

Category truth: the dental offices that book solid every week treat their website like the front desk. The rest treat it like a brochure for a building.

## What your dental site actually needs

Start at the top. A slim promo strip above the nav with the dated new patient special and the price. Below the nav, a coral emergency strip with the same-day promise, a tap-to-call link, and a Hablamos Español note when applicable. The nav: Services, Specials, Membership, Smile Gallery, Office Tour, Insurance, Contact. A book button stays loud on every scroll.

Specials is its own section with three dated cards: the new patient cleaning and exam at a real price, a free Invisalign consult, and a take-home whitening kit included with a new patient cleaning. Membership is the Ridgeview Smile Plan or your equivalent, with a monthly price, a benefits checklist (two cleanings included, x-rays included, percentage off everything else), and a single Join CTA.

Services break out into clear categories: General, Cosmetic, Restorative, Orthodontic, Sedation, Pediatric. Smile Gallery shows three real cases with photos and brief case notes. Office Tour names the technology (3D iTero scans, CEREC same-day crowns, intraoral cameras, sedation suite) on one column, comfort amenities (Netflix, weighted blankets, pediatric room) on the other. The hygiene team gets named with photos. Financing names CareCredit, Sunbit, Cherry, and in-house plans, with a specific monthly example on a real procedure. Multi-modal contact gives Call, Text, and Book Online as three equal cards with `tel:` and `sms:` schemes. Insurance names every carrier as a pill, not a sentence.

The footer carries the Texas dental license number, ADA and TDA membership, CDC and OSHA sterilization standards, accessibility, and the Hablamos Español flag. The whole page is mobile-first because that is where the chipped tooth gets searched.
