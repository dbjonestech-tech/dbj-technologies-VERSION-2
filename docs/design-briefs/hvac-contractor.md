## How HVAC customers actually find you

The HVAC customer is in pain. The AC died on the hottest day of August. The furnace is making a sound at 2am in January. They are not browsing. They are searching from a phone, standing in front of a broken unit, and they need to know three things: do you serve their zip code, can you come today, and will it bankrupt them.

What converts is specific. A phone number that is the loudest thing on the page. A 24/7 promise that is real, not aspirational. A service area with the actual cities you drive to, not twenty SEO pills. A Google review count above two thousand with the rating attached. BBB, NATE, and manufacturer credentials. A coupon visible on the first scroll, dated.

What loses the call is generic. A contact form instead of a phone number. "Available 24/7" with no phone visible. Stock photos of a generic technician. A review count below five hundred. No financing call-out on a $14,000 replacement. The customer hits back and dials the next contractor on the map.

## What most HVAC sites get wrong

I audit DFW HVAC sites every month. The patterns are stubborn.

The phone number is small, gray, and tucked into the header. The "Schedule Service" button leads to a contact form promising a 24-hour callback, which is not what someone with no AC in August needs. The hero photo is stock. The reviews section says "trusted by hundreds" without the count or the platform. The service area is a 4x5 grid of city pills that reads as SEO bait, not as a list of places the truck actually drives to.

Then there are the missing surfaces. No Special Offers page even though coupons are what HVAC customers click first. No Membership program even though annual maintenance plans are the highest-margin recurring revenue line. No Financing call-out even though most replacements are financed. No multi-modal contact (phone, text, book online) even though many customers would rather text a photo of the error code than call. No General Manager named, no dispatch named.

Category truth: the HVAC contractors that book solid in the heat dome treat their website like a dispatch desk. The rest treat it like a Yellow Pages ad.

## What your HVAC site actually needs

Start at the top. A slim seasonal promo strip above everything else (Spring tune-up, fall furnace check, holiday specials) that scrolls away. Below that, an orange emergency strip with the phone as a tap-to-call link. The nav: Services, Special Offers, Membership, Service Area, Reviews, Financing, Contact. Phone number lives in the nav as the loudest element on the strip.

Hero is full-bleed photography of a real branded van or a uniformed technician, not stock. The H1 names the pain. Below it, four trust badges: Google count with rating, BBB Accredited with year, NATE Certified Techs, manufacturer Factory Authorized status. Each badge has a glass treatment so the hero image reads through.

Special Offers is its own section with three dated coupons, each with real expiration dates. Membership is a branded section (Comfort Club or your brand's equivalent) with an annual price, a benefits checklist (priority dispatch, two seasonal tune-ups, percentage off repairs, no after-hours fees, two-year warranty, transferable on home sale), and a single Join the Club CTA.

Service Area is three or four regional zone cards, not pills. Each zone lists the cities you actually drive to. Multi-modal contact is its own strip: Call (24/7 live answer), Text (photo-of-the-unit in one thread), Book Online (self-schedule with 60-second confirmation). All three surface the same number through `tel:` and `sms:` schemes so a tap from a phone does the right thing.

The footer carries the state license number (TACLA in Texas), BBB rating, manufacturer authorizations, NATE certification, links to Google / Facebook / Nextdoor / BBB review platforms, hours by day, and an emergency tap-to-call line. Every page is mobile-first because that is where customers find you.
